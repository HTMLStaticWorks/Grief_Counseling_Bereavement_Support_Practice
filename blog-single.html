Understanding the Waves of Grief

By Dr. Sarah Jenkins · Oct 12 · 4 min read

When we first experience a profound loss, we often expect grief to be a linear process—a steep climb that eventually plateaus into acceptance. But grief rarely follows a straight line.

More often, grief feels like standing in the ocean. At first, the waves are towering and relentless. They knock you off your feet, leaving you gasping for air. Over time, the waves may become smaller and further apart, but they can still catch you off guard, triggered by a song, a smell, or an anniversary.

The Myth of Closure

Society often emphasizes the idea of "closure," suggesting that there is a defined endpoint to mourning. However, in our practice, we have found that the goal is not to close the door on the past, but to find a way to carry the love forward.

When a wave of grief hits unexpectedly, your instinct might be to fight it or suppress the emotion. Instead, try to practice gentle curiosity. Acknowledge the feeling. Say to yourself, "I am feeling a wave of deep sadness right now, and that makes sense because I loved them deeply."

"You do not have to move on; you just have to gently move forward."

Practical Steps for the Hard Days

  • Breathe: It sounds simple, but grief often causes shallow breathing. Take three slow, deep breaths.
  • Reach Out: You don't have to explain how you feel. A simple text to a trusted friend saying, "It's a hard day," is enough.
  • Rest: Grief is physically exhausting. Give yourself permission to cancel plans and rest.

Remember, there is no right or wrong way to grieve. Your journey is your own, and honoring your pace is the most compassionate thing you can do for yourself.
